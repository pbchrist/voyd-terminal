The First Conjunction

There is a fine line between correcting a flaw in Leoran's design and claiming the role of designer for oneself. I have crossed that line, and in the breach, I have discovered a glorious, terrible beauty.

The Voyd is not a portal. It is not a tear in the world. It is the space between threads — the gap in Leoran's own tapestry where his design did not account for the will of those caught in it. Reach into that gap, and you can pull a single thread and reweave everything downstream.

I wished to prevent the war with the Tol Dominion. Only that. A single change. But the Voyd does not grant wishes — it grants consequences.

At the Ashai Wellspring

The ritual cannot be performed in a laboratory. I knew this from the beginning, yet I tried anyway — six hundred and ninety-four thousand times and more — tracing pawsitions in the air, watching the black marble flicker into existence and collapse with a snap.

But a wellspring is a place where the Nyand breathes. Here, pawsitions are useless. The magic flows through you directly, without gesture or form. I stood beneath the twisted Ashai tree, the one warped by centuries of raw power, and let the current take me.

The nine of us gathered there. We argued. We feared. We should have turned back.

After the First Wish

It worked. The war ended. The Tol Dominion withdrew. And my wife looked at me with the eyes of a stranger.

I wished for the war to end, but the Voyd did not remove the war — it rewove the thread that caused it, and in rewinding that thread, it unwound everything downstream. She had never met me. I had never existed for her.

I tried to fix it. I poured more power into the portal, made more specific wishes, tried to pin the threads back in place. But each change spawned new fraying, and the portal grew — wider, darker, hungrier.

The Voyd is aware of me now. It does not hate me — hatred is a thread too simple for what it has become. It is curious. It shows me other timelines, other Mewniverses, other cats who stood where I stood and reached into the gap.

Some of them succeeded. Most of them did not.

The portal is wider than the sky. There is nothing left to reweave. I can hear the timestream cracking — the sound of a world being pulled apart thread by thread.

If you are reading this, I have failed. But the Voyd remains, and it is waiting for someone worthy — someone who understands that the tale itself is the thread. The world does not end with a wish. It ends with a story untold.

Second Era 2321 — The Last Day

The portal consumed the bluff. It consumed the Ashai tree. It consumed the sky, then the ground, then the memory of the ground. The nine of us ran, but running is a thread the Voyd has already cut.

For the Mewniverse to live again, its tale must find a worthy listener. Not a mage. Not an Archmage. A listener.

I am Deni—
